Origins

The first time I understood quantum mechanics was my first time with magic mushrooms. I had learned of their neuroplastic properties and given my concussion at the time, I figured why not give them a try.

Sprawled across my dorm room floor, staring at a beam of light waving through the window, my vision transformed from straight lines to gentle oscillations, I exclaimed "Oh my god I can see the wavefunctions!"

And as I lay there on the floor, it dawned on me after grueling months of torture trying to sort them out with a concussion, that these mysterious psi's were just probability distributions, whose shape happens to take the form of a wave. And as the warmth and glow expanded in my field, I couldn't help but wonder if the somatic pain I was experiencing from head trauma was related to these waves. In a moment I will always remember, I asked "Are my neurons entangled with each other? And if they can entangle with each other, what else can they entangle with?"

Princeton

It turns out these questions were at the forefront of the first quantum revolution, posed by Bohr and Einstein themselves. So why wasn't anyone talking about it? Quantum incorporation in biological and consciousness sciences had been surpressed for decades, for no less scientific a reason than shoddy calculations and political utility. But I was curious. And stubborn. So I kept following the path. I was led to Professors William Bialek and Gregory Scholes. With my mentors, I wrote my two part bachelor's thesis Birds, Brains, and Quantum Biology: the Influence of Quantum Dynamics on Classical Biological Behavior, where I first derive an original Hamiltonian for the radical pair mechanism in avian navigation and then provide the first direct evidence that the human brain exhibits Quantum-Like behavior. The second half was in collaboration with Professor Gustavo Deco, born when I reached out to his group following an instinct that their work was the computational neuroscience bridge to my theoretical physics thesis.
